The Speed Trap

Why speed without foundation creates the illusion of progress

January 15, 2026 Engineering 8 min read

I see this pattern repeat across projects.

A team starts fast. AI tools, modern stack, motivated engineers. The first version ships in weeks. Everyone celebrates.

Then users show up. Edge cases appear. The payment flow breaks. The data sync fails silently. Features that worked in demo crash under real load.

Suddenly the team that was "moving fast" is spending months fixing things. Not building new features. Just paying back the debt from shipping without foundation.

The Pattern

Here's how it typically unfolds:

Week 1-4: The honeymoon. Everything is new. The stack is modern. AI tools are generating code faster than anyone expected. Features ship daily. The demo looks amazing.

Week 5-8: The cracks appear. A bug shows up in production. The fix breaks something else. Someone asks "why did we build it this way?" and nobody knows. The original context is gone.

Month 3-6: The slowdown. Every new feature takes longer than expected. Simple changes require understanding complex dependencies. The team spends more time investigating than building.

Month 6+: The reckoning. Leadership asks why the team is "slow now." The team isn't slow. They're doing the work that should have been done upfront.

Speed without foundation creates the illusion of progress. Then reality catches up.

Why This Happens

The speed trap exists because of a timing mismatch.

The benefits of speed are immediate. Ship fast, get feedback, iterate quickly. This is genuinely valuable. It's how startups win.

The costs of speed are delayed. Technical debt, knowledge gaps, maintenance burden. These don't show up in the first demo. They show up six months later when you're trying to change something.

This timing mismatch creates a dangerous incentive. The person who ships fast gets praised today. The person who deals with the consequences gets blamed tomorrow.

The Real Cost

I've tracked this across multiple projects. Here's what I consistently see:

Time spent understanding code vs writing code: In well-documented systems, it's about 30/70. In systems built for speed without foundation, it's 70/30. Engineers spend most of their time figuring out what the code does, not improving it.

Bug fix time: In systems with clear architecture, a typical bug takes 1-2 hours to fix. In systems built fast without foundation, the same bug can take 1-2 days. Not because the fix is harder, but because finding the right place to fix it takes forever.

Onboarding time: New engineers in well-documented systems are productive in 1-2 weeks. In speed-trap systems, it's 1-2 months. Sometimes longer.

How to Escape

If you're already in the speed trap, here's how to get out:

1. Stop adding features temporarily. I know this is hard. Leadership wants features. Users want features. But you can't outrun technical debt. It compounds. The longer you wait, the harder it gets.

2. Document what exists. Not comprehensive documentation. Start with the parts that cause the most confusion. Write down why things were built the way they were. Future you will thank present you.

3. Create boundaries. Identify the messiest parts of the system. Put clear interfaces around them. You don't have to rewrite everything. Just contain the chaos.

4. Make it easier to do things right. If writing tests is hard, make it easier. If deploying safely is hard, make it easier. Remove friction from the right behaviors.

How to Avoid It

Prevention is better than cure. Here's how to move fast without falling into the trap:

Ship fast, but document decisions. You don't need comprehensive docs. You need decision logs. Why did we choose this approach? What did we consider and reject? This takes 10 minutes and saves 10 hours later.

Invest 20% in foundation. For every sprint focused on features, spend 20% on infrastructure, tooling, and documentation. This slows you down 20% today but speeds you up 200% in six months.

Hire for curiosity. The engineers who ask "why?" are the ones who build things that last. The engineers who just want to ship fast will optimize for today at the expense of tomorrow.

Real speed comes from clarity, not from typing faster.
