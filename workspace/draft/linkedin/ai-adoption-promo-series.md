## Post 1: The Main Hook

**Angle:** Core thesis - workflow before tools
**Best for:** First promotion, highest reach potential
**Publish:** Personal account

---

AI adoption is a workflow and skills decision. Tools follow.

Most organizations get this backwards.

They buy a tool. Mandate "use AI." Measure prompts per day.

Then wonder why quality drops, context leaks, and nobody trusts the output.

We see this pattern across industries:

→ Marketing: AI writes content, nobody reviews, brand voice drifts
→ Finance: AI generates reports, nobody validates, errors compound
→ Operations: AI automates tasks, nobody owns exceptions, chaos
→ Engineering: AI writes code, nobody understands it, technical debt accelerates

The common thread: tools without workflow design equals faster mistakes.

AI is a speed multiplier. If your process is broken, AI multiplies the breakage.

The framework that works:

1. Map the workflow before selecting tools
2. Build skills before scaling usage
3. Establish guardrails before pursuing speed
4. Select tools that fit the workflow

If you cannot draw the workflow, you cannot automate it.

## Post 2: The Skills Angle

**Angle:** The 5 human skills that determine AI productivity
**Best for:** Mid-week engagement, practical focus
**Publish:** Personal account

---

AI tools don't determine productivity.

Human skills do.

We've seen teams where 20% of people become 5x more productive with AI. Same tool. Same training. Same access.

The difference is 5 skills:

**1. Problem Framing**

Turn vague into testable.

"Make this better" produces garbage.
"Reduce response time from 4 hours to 1 hour while maintaining satisfaction scores" produces results.

**2. Decomposition**

Split work into verifiable steps.

"Write a quarterly report" is too big. AI will guess wrong.
Break it into: gather data → verify → analyze → verify → draft → verify → format.

**3. Evaluation**

Know what good looks like before you see AI output.

Teams without this skill accept plausible-looking output. Plausible is not correct.

**4. Correction**

Fix the 20%, don't regenerate.

Teams that regenerate instead of correct waste enormous time and never improve.

**5. Systems Thinking**

Make it easier next time.

Build templates. Document what worked. Create a prompt library. Compound your learning.

Tool training is necessary but not sufficient.

These skills determine whether AI multiplies your capability or your mistakes.

## Post 3: The Warning Signs

**Angle:** How to know if AI adoption is failing
**Best for:** Problem-aware audience, diagnostic value
**Publish:** Personal account

---

5 signs your AI adoption is failing:

**1. Quality complaints increase after AI introduction**

AI should improve quality or maintain it. If customers notice degradation, something is broken.

The workflow or guardrails aren't working.

**2. "AI made a mistake" becomes an excuse**

AI doesn't make mistakes. Processes make mistakes.

If people blame AI for errors, accountability isn't clear.

**3. Nobody can explain how AI output gets validated**

Ask your team: "How do you know this AI output is correct?"

If they can't articulate a clear answer, validation isn't happening.

**4. Tool switching every few months**

If you keep evaluating new tools looking for the "right" solution, the problem isn't tools.

No tool will solve a workflow problem.

**5. Shadow AI emerges**

People use personal AI tools because official ones don't fit their workflow.

This signals the implementation doesn't match how work actually gets done.

These aren't AI problems. They're workflow design problems.

Switching tools won't fix them. Redesigning the workflow will.
