The engineers who ship fastest aren't the best coders.
Here's the skill they actually use.

An engineer gets assigned a big ticket. Estimates quickly without clarifying requirements. Starts working. Hits complexity. Ticket sits in "In Progress" for 2 weeks.

Everyone waits for someone else to unblock them.

Here's the reality: Only the person doing the work has enough insight to cut scope.

You can't wait for a PM to simplify it for you. You can't wait for someone to tell you what to remove. You're the one who sees what's actually hard and what's not necessary for v1.

Cut scope aggressively to ensure you ship something.

But here's the thing: You can't cut scope if you don't have clarity on what you're building.

Before cutting, get your MVC (Minimum Viable Clarity):
→ WHO is this for?
→ WHAT problem does it solve?
→ WHY does it matter now?

Without MVC, every feature feels essential. With it, cutting becomes obvious.

If you're on an Agile team, learn SPIDR to split stories:
→ Spike (research unknowns first)
→ Path (happy path vs edge cases)
→ Interface (simple UI first)
→ Data (hardcode before dynamic)
→ Rules (core rules first, exceptions later)

When you commit to a deadline:

→ Make it the absolute priority
→ Learn to say no to features that aren't essential
→ Start fast and iterate early to give optionality
→ Ship ugly but working over perfect but invisible

The goal isn't to deliver everything in the spec. The goal is to ship something valuable to the customer.

"What's the smallest version of this that actually solves the problem?"

Ask this question every day until it ships.

I've seen teams go from 2-week cycles stuck in progress to shipping daily. The difference wasn't velocity or talent. It was scope discipline.

The best engineers I know cut scope before anyone asks them to.

How do you handle scope creep on your team?
