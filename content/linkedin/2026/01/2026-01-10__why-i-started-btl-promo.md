I looked at my team's code.

The problem wasn't them. It was me.

One project. Gaps everywhere.

Missing architecture. No standards. Code that worked.

No comments. No docs. No patterns.

Duplication everywhere. Hard to extend. Impossible to reuse.

I hadn't given enough clarity upfront. I assumed they understood my intent.

They didn't.

That project became a mirror.

It showed me how much knowledge lives only in my head. 15 years. 50+ projects. Patterns I follow instinctively but never documented. Lessons from failure I never made explicit.

Tech debt doesn't come from speed. It comes from skipping clarity.

I've learned the difference between skipping foundations and keeping things simple.

You don't need complex architecture. You need enough clarity that people know what they're building on.

Foundation first is intentional. Simple. Clear. Fast.

If my own team can't access what I know, the knowledge is wasted.

So I started writing. I call it Builds That Last.

What I've learned from building systems that had to work:

→ Why foundations enable speed
→ How to use AI without accelerating technical debt
→ What engineering leadership looks like in the messy middle

Everyone ships fast. Few build to last.

What about you? Ever had a clarity problem disguised as a team problem?

Hoang Le
Founder, Builds That Last
